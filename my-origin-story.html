The Origin Story

23 MAR 2026
6 MIN READ
BY STR1X3VO

Every gamer has a moment. The exact second the controller - or in my case, the handheld - clicked into your hands and something just made sense. Mine came at four or five years old, sat with a Game Boy, thumbs already learning the language that would end up defining pretty much everything that followed.

It started with a Game Boy

No fancy gaming PC. No RGB setup. Just a grey brick with a green tinted screen and a pair of AA batteries that never lasted long enough. Mario was the gateway - platforming through worlds that felt enormous when you're small enough to fit in a trolley seat. Then came Need for Speed. Then every other OG title I could get my hands on.

There's something about that era of gaming that just hits different. No internet connection required. No day one patches. No battle passes. Just a cartridge, a screen, and you. The games were hard, the saves were precious, and finishing one actually meant something.

Turning 18 - the first real build

The day I turned 18 I went straight out and built my first proper PC. Not bought - built. And I mean that literally, because sourcing the parts meant hitting basically every shop I could think of. Currys PC World for some bits. CEX for others. The local market. Amazon for whatever was left. Wherever the best price was, that's where I went.

It wasn't the most elegant process. But there's something deeply satisfying about putting a machine together from parts you hunted down yourself rather than just clicking buy on a prebuilt. Every component felt earned.

The cases though - that's where I kept tripping up. Went through three different cases before I found one I was happy with. Not because anything was broken, just because I kept looking at it and thinking nah, that's not right. The inside of a PC matters as much as what it can do. If you're going to spend hours sat next to it, it needs to look right.

// On building your first PC Don't buy a prebuilt. Hunt the parts, compare prices, use CEX, use the market, use Amazon. You'll end up with better specs for less money and you'll actually understand what's inside your machine.

The sim racing rabbit hole

A year or two ago I picked up a steering wheel setup from CEX - GBP 150 all in, postage included. At the time it felt like a decent gamble. Turns out it was probably one of the better purchases I've made.

That's a Thrustmaster TMX Pro with the T3PA three pedal add-on. Force feedback, proper clutch, the lot - for the price of a night out. Sim racing completely changed how I game. Assetto Corsa with Pure and CSP went from looking like a racing game to looking like a film. iRacing scratches the competitive itch. Euro Truck Simulator is weirdly therapeutic after a long day.

If you're even slightly into driving games and you haven't tried a wheel setup, you're missing out. The difference is night and day.

FiveM - the accidental developer arc

I never planned to become a developer. That one's entirely my mate's fault.

He kept getting ripped off. Paying developers for FiveM server scripts, getting half-finished work or nothing at all, watching money disappear into thin air. I got tired of watching it happen. So instead of finding another developer to trust, I just figured I'd learn it myself.

Turns out Lua isn't that scary once you stop being scared of it. Neither is JavaScript. Or CSS. You just start breaking things, figure out why they broke, and slowly build something that works. Do that enough times and you're a developer - not because someone handed you a certificate, but because you actually built things that real people used on a real server.

That's the only kind of experience that actually counts.

The content creation bit

This one's simple. I have a lot of spare time, I'm always gaming anyway, and one day I thought - why not record it

Started posting VODs to TikTok. No grand plan, no content strategy, no brand deals lined up. Just gameplay, commentary, whatever I was actually doing. Sim racing mostly - Assetto Corsa, iRacing, B-road bashing in a hot hatch with the Pure mod making everything look cinematic. The kind of driving content that makes you want to load up the game yourself.

The website came later. Once you've got content, you want somewhere to put it that you actually own. Not an algorithm's platform, not a subscription service - somewhere that exists because you made it exist. So I built one. Off my bedroom PC. For GBP 1.97.

// Where it's going More content. More posts. A Raspberry Pi taking over hosting duties any day now. And somewhere down the line, whatever comes next - I'll figure it out the same way I figured out everything else. Just start, break things, and keep going.

That's the whole story so far. Game Boy at four to self-hosted website, three PC cases deep, GBP 150 sim rig and a FiveM dev career nobody asked for. Not bad.
